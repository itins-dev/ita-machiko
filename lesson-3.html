Lesson 3 — Numbers & Money

Prices · Counting · Paying · Beginner

⏱ 30 minutes 🎯 Beginner ✈️ Italy-bound
Lesson objective: By the end, she can count to 100, understand prices out loud, ask how much something costs, and handle basic money exchanges at a market, café, or shop.
1

Warm-up — Quick Review

Recap lessons 1 & 2, prime for numbers
3 min

Run a 60-second flashcard drill on key phrases from lessons 1 and 2. Then ask: "You're at a market stall and the vendor says a price. How do you understand it?"

This creates the motivation — numbers aren't abstract, they're what she'll hear every time she pays for anything.

Tens: 30–100 & How to Combine

The pattern that unlocks all other numbers
6 min
30trenta
40quaranta
50cinquanta
60sessanta
70settanta
80ottanta
90novanta
100cento
The rule: tens + units run together — trenta + due = trentadue (32). When the unit starts with a vowel (1, 8), drop the final vowel of the ten: trentauno → trentuno, trentaotto → trentotto.

Money & Prices

Everything she needs to pay for things
7 min

Quanto costa?How much does it cost?

Solo contantiCash only

Italy still has many cash-only places, especially at markets and small bars. Solo contanti is something she'll hear — make sure it registers.
Price drill You say a price aloud in Italian (Sono dodici euro e cinquanta). She writes the number. Then reverse: you write a number, she says it in Italian. Do 5–6 rounds.
5

Putting It Together — Market Role-Play

Numbers in a real transaction
4 min
Scenario She's at a food market. She picks up some fruit, asks the price, listens to the answer, asks if they take card, and pays.
Sample exchange
Student: Buongiorno! Quanto costano queste arance?
Vendor (you): Sono due euro al chilo.
Student: Un chilo, per favore. Posso pagare con carta?
Vendor: No, mi dispiace — solo contanti.
Student: Va bene, ecco a lei. (handing over cash)
Tip: ecco a lei = "here you go" — a very natural handover phrase worth teaching.
6

Wrap-up & Homework

Cement the numbers before they fade
2 min

Quick fire: you call out numbers at random between 1–100, she says them in Italian. Then flip — you say Italian, she says the digit.

  • 🔢 Count from 1–20 out loud every morning — takes 30 seconds and locks them in within a week.
  • 🛒 Next time she's shopping, mentally say the prices in Italian. Even in her home country, any price tag becomes practice.
  • 📱 Duolingo numbers module — 5 minutes daily. The repetition matters more than the duration.

Teacher Notes

  • Don't drill all of 1–100 sequentially — it's boring and won't stick. Use random numbers and price-reading instead.
  • Prioritise 1–20 and the tens. The combining rule handles everything else.
  • Italian prices use a comma for decimals: €12,50 is said dodici euro e cinquanta. Point this out — she'll see it on every price tag.
  • If she finishes early: practice telling the time — Che ore sono? Sono le tre. It uses the same numbers and is immediately useful.
